# Camp page templates

Load this when writing a specific page. It gives camp-shaped headline patterns, a section-by-section layout per page type, the calls to action that fit a camp, and the objections to answer. Write the actual words in camp-voice, and fill every price, date, quote, and photo from real facts or mark them as `[placeholders]`.

## Headline patterns for camps

Pick the one that fits the page. All of them favor a plain, specific line over a clever one.

- **Type, place, ages:** "A traditional overnight camp on a lake in [region], for ages 8 to 14."
- **For this audience:** "Summer day camp in [town] for ages 5 to 12, built around the outdoors."
- **The honest outcome:** "A first summer away from home, with counselors trained to help kids settle."
- **The plain question:** "Looking for a soccer camp near [town] this summer?"
- **Proof up front:** "In our 41st summer on [lake]."

## Calls to action for camps

Name the action and what the parent gets. Match it to how ready they are.

- Researching: "See session dates," "Check availability," "Request the brochure," "Book a tour."
- Nearly ready: "Join the waitlist," "Ask us a question."
- Ready: "Start your application," "Register for [session]."

Avoid "Submit," "Sign up," and "Learn more."

## Homepage

The job is to orient a parent who has never heard of you, fast, and send them to the next step. It has to work for a parent still researching and one ready to book.

1. **Hero:** headline (what, who, where), subhead (session lengths, dates, what is particular), one primary button, and a single trust line (accreditation, or "since [year]").
2. **Proof line:** accreditation, years running, how many families return, or one short parent quote.
3. **Programs or age groups:** a short block for each, with the key facts and a link to its own page.
4. **What a session looks like:** concrete and photo-led, not a list of adjectives.
5. **Trust and safety:** ratios, staff training and screening, accreditation, with a link to a fuller page if you have one.
6. **A couple of parent quotes**, attributed.
7. **Final call to action.**

## Program or session page

The job is to help a parent interested in this specific program decide to register or inquire.

1. **Hero:** the program name, place, and ages.
2. **The essentials, up top:** dates, ages, price, what is included, and where. Parents scan for these first, so do not bury them.
3. **What the child does:** the real activities and a typical day for this program.
4. **Trust for this program:** ratios, the staff who run it, and how safety is handled.
5. **Objections relevant to this program** (see the bank below).
6. **A parent quote** from a family who did this program, if you have one.
7. **Call to action:** register, check availability, or join the waitlist.

## Enrollment or registration page

The job is to make registering feel simple and safe, and to remove anything that makes a parent hesitate.

1. **A clear heading** that says what they are about to do.
2. **The steps, in plain order:** register, pay the deposit, complete the forms.
3. **Price and payment, stated plainly:** total, deposit, installment options, camperships, and sibling or early-bird discounts.
4. **The refund and cancellation policy**, easy to find.
5. **What happens next:** what the parent receives after registering, and who to contact with questions.
6. **A short form** that asks only for what you need at this step.
7. **Call to action:** start the application or register.

## About or our-story page

The job is to build trust and a sense of who you are.

1. **The real story:** who started the camp, how long it has run, and what it stands for, in the camp's own voice.
2. **The people:** the director and key staff, briefly, with relevant credentials.
3. **What the camp believes about kids and summer**, said in specific terms, not a generic mission statement.
4. **Photos** of the real place and people.
5. **A link back** to programs or enrollment.

## FAQ: the objection bank

Answer the questions parents actually hesitate over, phrased the way they would ask. This content also helps AI assistants recommend you (see camp-aeo), so it does double duty.

- How much does it cost, and is financial aid available?
- Is my child the right age, and will they fit in?
- What if my child gets homesick?
- How do you keep children safe? (ratios, staff screening, health care on site)
- What is the food like, and can you handle allergies and dietary needs?
- What does a typical day look like?
- What is your refund and cancellation policy?
- What should we pack, and how do drop-off and pickup work?

## Writing the sections well

- **Proof:** specific and attributed. "Maya's mom, a returning family since 2021" carries more than "a parent." Never invent a quote; mark it `[parent quote]` if you do not have a real one yet.
- **A day at camp:** walk through the hours in concrete detail. That persuades more than any adjective.
- **Photos:** real ones of this camp, with a caption saying what is happening.
- **Price:** show it, or give a clear range. A parent who cannot find the cost usually leaves.
