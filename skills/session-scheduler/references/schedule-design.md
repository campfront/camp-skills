# Camp schedule design: samples, tables, and arcs

Load this when building a schedule. It has a sample day, the block-length and ratio conventions, the themed-week and season arcs, and the developmental checklist. Treat the timings as conventions to adapt, and the ratios as guidance to confirm against the camp's own jurisdiction.

## A sample overnight camp day (with the energy arc)

This is a starting shape, not a template to copy. Adjust to the camp's meal times, facilities, and ages.

```
7:30   Wake-up, cabin tidy
8:00   Flag and announcements
8:15   Breakfast
9:00   Activity block 1   (higher energy: sports, waterfront, adventure)
10:15  Transition
10:30  Activity block 2   (skill or choice)
11:45  Free time / wash up
12:15  Lunch
1:00   Rest hour          (quiet, low-stimulation: the deliberate dip)
2:00   Activity block 3   (creative or calmer: arts, nature, free swim)
3:15   Transition + snack
3:45   Activity block 4   (the physical rebound: the hardest active block)
5:00   Free time / choice
6:00   Dinner
7:00   Evening program    (see the evening-program skill)
8:30   Wind-down, cabin time
9:00   Lights-out (younger), later for older
```

Day camp differs: build around arrival and pickup windows, keep a calmer pre-pickup wind-down, and you usually drop the rest hour and the evening program.

## Block length by age (convention)

A block can only run as long as the group's attention holds.

| Age | Block length |
|-----|--------------|
| 5 to 8 | 30 to 45 min |
| 9 to 11 | 45 to 60 min |
| 12+ | 60 to 90 min (project or skill work) |

Plan a real 10 to 15 minute transition between blocks at every age.

## Supervision ratios (ACA guidance, confirm your own)

These are the American Camp Association's published guidance figures. They are not universal law. Confirm the ratios the camp must follow with its jurisdiction and accreditor (for example, Ontario camps follow Regulation 503/17 plus the OCA).

| Age | Resident (overnight) | Day camp |
|-----|----------------------|----------|
| 4 to 5 | 1:5 | 1:6 |
| 6 to 8 | 1:6 | 1:8 |
| 9 to 14 | 1:8 | 1:10 |
| 15 to 17 | 1:10 | 1:12 |

Tighten for higher-risk activities and for campers with additional support needs. Every concurrent block needs the staffing math checked against the ratio for that age.

## The themed week (a five-day arc)

A theme works when it is broad enough to feed several activity types and builds to a finish.

```
Day 1  Reveal the theme; an easy, inclusive kickoff so everyone is in
Day 2  A creative project that runs across the week
Day 3  A team challenge or wide game on the theme
Day 4  Build toward the finale; rehearse or qualify
Day 5  The culminating event, then a calm close
```

Pick the theme from the campers' age outward, not the director's taste. Brief counselors about a week ahead so supplies arrive in time.

## The season arc

- Open strong: a high-energy, crowd-pleasing week (a color war or all-camp games).
- Vary active and creative themes week to week.
- Place the quieter themes mid-summer, when camper and staff energy dips.
- Keep theme variety across sessions if families repeat.

## The eight features of a positive developmental setting

A strong schedule meets these (Eccles and Gootman). Use as a checklist:

1. Physical and psychological safety
2. Appropriate structure (clear, not rigid)
3. Supportive relationships
4. Opportunities to belong
5. Positive social norms
6. Support for efficacy and mattering
7. Opportunities for skill building
8. Integration with family and community

## Inclusion in the schedule

- Keep routines predictable, and use a visual schedule for campers who need one.
- Build in a quiet or sensory-regulation space campers can use.
- Design blocks with more than one way to take part, so a camper can opt into a lower-stimulation version rather than only in or out.
- Make sure competitive structures do not leave the same campers or cabins chronically losing.
