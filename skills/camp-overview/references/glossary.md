# Summer camp glossary

Camp-specific terms used across the US and Canadian summer camp world. Many vary by region or by individual camp, and common variants are noted. This leans toward traditional overnight camp, where the vocabulary is richest, so not every term applies at a day camp or a specialty program.

## Camp types

- Day camp: campers attend during the day and go home each night.
- Overnight camp: campers live on site for the length of a session. Also called resident or sleepaway camp.
- Coed camp: boys and girls attend together, usually sleeping in separate cabins but sharing activities and meals.
- Single-gender camp: serves only boys or only girls. Two single-gender camps run by one operator that share some events are often called brother and sister camps.
- Traditional or general camp: a broad mix of activities rather than a single focus.
- Specialty camp: built around one focus, such as sports, arts, STEM, riding, or language.
- Tripping or wilderness camp: centered on backcountry trips, canoeing, or hiking.
- Family camp: sessions where whole families attend together.
- Travel or teen tour camp: older campers travel rather than stay at one site.
- Inclusion or special needs camp: facilities, staff, and program adapted for campers with disabilities or medical, physical, or developmental needs. "Special needs" is still widely used in the field, though "inclusion" and "camps serving campers with disabilities" are now often preferred.

## People and roles

- Camper: a child or teen attending camp.
- Counselor: front-line staff who lead or live with a group of campers. A cabin or bunk counselor is assigned to a living group. (Spelled counsellor in Canada.)
- Co: a counselor's co-counselor, the partner who shares a cabin or group.
- Specialist: staff who run a specific activity area, such as waterfront, arts, riding, or climbing.
- CIT and LIT: counselor-in-training and leader-in-training, older teens in a development program that bridges from camper toward a staff role. Some camps use other names, such as CILT, for the same idea.
- JC: junior counselor, usually an entry-level paid staff role a step below a full counselor, distinct from a CIT or LIT who is not yet staff.
- Division head, group leader, or unit head: oversees a group of cabins, usually by age.
- Program director: designs and runs the activity schedule and special events.
- Camp director: runs the camp's program, staff, and operations, and at independent camps is often the owner. May be seasonal or year-round.
- Assistant director: supports the director across daily operations.
- Waterfront director: runs and supervises all water activities.
- Lifeguard and lookout: certified guards watch swimmers; a lookout is an extra set of eyes who is not certified.
- Camp nurse or health center staff: provide medical care on site; larger camps may also have a doctor.
- Kitchen or work crew: staff who prepare and serve meals and clean up.
- OD (on duty): the staff member responsible for campers during the evening and overnight hours while others are off.
- International staff: seasonal staff from abroad, common at resident camps (see J-1).

## Places and facilities

- Cabin or bunk: a camper living group and the building it sleeps in. "Bunk" often also means the group of campers itself, and elsewhere a bunk bed.
- Unit, division, or village: a cluster of cabins, usually grouped by age.
- Tent platform: a raised wooden deck that a tent sits on, used in place of cabins at some camps (which may also use yurts or tipis).
- Bunkline or cabin line: the row or area where camper cabins sit.
- Dining hall or mess hall: where camp eats together.
- Health center, infirmary, or health lodge: the camp's medical building.
- Waterfront: the lake, pool, or pond area and its docks.
- Camp store, canteen, or trading post: where campers get snacks and supplies, usually charged to a prepaid account rather than cash. Names vary by region.
- Bathhouse: a shared building for toilets, sinks, and showers when these are not attached to the cabins.
- Flagpole: the gathering spot for morning and evening flag ceremonies and announcements.
- Lodge or rec hall: indoor space for assemblies, evening programs, and activities.
- Ropes or challenge course: high and low elements for climbing and teamwork. A climbing tower or wall is a built structure that simulates rock climbing.
- The blob: a large inflatable on the water that launches one camper into the air when another jumps onto it.
- Counselor or staff lounge: a space reserved for staff during time off.

## The daily routine

- Season: how long a camp runs over the summer, usually split into sessions.
- Session: the block of dates a camper attends, from about one to eight weeks; camps often run several across a summer.
- Changeover or turnover: the gap between sessions when one group leaves and the next arrives, and a busy cleaning and prep window for staff.
- Reveille: the morning wake-up call.
- Flag or colors: the gathering at the flagpole before meals to sing, hear announcements, and raise or lower the flag.
- Morning assembly: a post-breakfast gathering for announcements, songs, and skits.
- Activity period: a scheduled block for a specific activity.
- Rest hour, cabin time, or siesta: a quiet midday break in the cabin, sometimes called FOB (feet on bunk).
- Zones or free time: unstructured time when campers choose what to do within a set area.
- Evening program: the organized activity after dinner, such as a wide game, campfire, or theme night.
- Cabin chat or devotions: an end-of-day reflection with the group; faith camps often call the reflective version devotions.
- Lights out or taps: the end-of-day call and bedtime.
- ETB (early to bed): a night when campers, and sometimes staff, turn in earlier than usual.
- Off: a staff member's scheduled time off, when they are not responsible for campers.
- Mail call: the distribution of letters and packages, often with a package list read at a meal.
- Cabin cleanup, chores, or kapers: the daily tidy of the cabin, sometimes scored as a cabin inspection.
- Bug juice: camp slang for the sugary powdered drink served at meals.

## Activities and traditions

- Color war (also colour war, Olympics, or at Jewish camps Maccabiah): a multi-day, all-camp competition that splits camp into teams, often by color. A version where teams represent countries is sometimes called a country or nations war.
- Fake break: a staged false start to a big event like color war, played as a prank before the real reveal.
- Visiting day or parents' day: when families come to camp partway through a session.
- Banquet: a special meal or ceremony marking the end of a session.
- Opening and closing campfire: the first and last campfires of a session, often with skits and awards.
- Gaga or gaga ball: a dodgeball variant played by hand in an enclosed pit.
- Nine square: a raised-frame game similar to four square but with nine players.
- Theme day: a day built around a costume or activity theme.
- Skit and talent show: camper or cabin performances.
- Camp song, chant, and cheer: songs and call-and-response chants tied to the camp, sung at meals, fires, and between teams.
- Intercamp: a friendly competition or meet-up between different camps.
- Care package: a parcel from home; many camps restrict these or ban food.
- Camp trunk or footlocker: the large case campers pack their gear in.
- Camp bubble: the sense that camp is its own world, cut off from outside news and routines.

## Waterfront and trips

- Swim test or swim check: an assessment at the start of a session that sets where a camper is allowed to swim.
- Buddy system: pairing swimmers and counting them off during a buddy check so everyone is accounted for.
- General, instructional, and free swim: recreational, lesson, and open swim periods.
- Out-trip or tripping: organized trips off site, from day hikes to multi-day expeditions. Anywhere off the property is out-of-camp (OOC).

## Enrollment and money

- Enrollment or registration: signing a camper up for a session.
- Re-enrollment: a returning camper signing up for next year, often starting during the current season.
- Open versus closed enrollment: whether new families can join freely or only at set times or by invitation.
- Campership: financial aid or a scholarship covering part or all of tuition.
- Early bird: a discounted rate for registering early.
- Sibling discount: a reduced rate for additional children from one family.
- Camper account: prepaid money a camper spends at the camp store.
- Deposit: an upfront payment that reserves a spot, usually applied toward tuition.
- Balance: the tuition still owed after the deposit, due before camp and sometimes paid in installments.
- Refund and cancellation policy: the terms setting what a family gets back, and when, if they cancel.
- Waitlist: the queue of families wanting a place in a full session, drawn on as spots open.
- Withdrawal: a camper pulling out of a session after registering.
- Camp advisor or referral service: a usually free-to-family service that helps parents choose a camp and is paid a commission by the camp.
- Camp fair: an event where camps exhibit to prospective families.

## Bodies, safety, and staffing

- ACA: American Camp Association, the main US accrediting and professional body.
- CCA: Canadian Camps Association (also seen as the Canadian Camping Association), a federation of provincial associations.
- OCA: Ontario Camps Association, the largest Canadian provincial body.
- Accreditation: a voluntary quality standard a camp meets and is reviewed against.
- Camper-to-counselor ratio: the required number of campers per staff member, set by accreditation or regulation.
- Mandated reporter: a staff member legally required to report suspected child abuse.
- Background check: screening of staff for criminal and abuse history, often required by states, insurers, or accreditation.
- Staff orientation or training: the period before campers arrive when staff are hired, trained, and prepared.
- J-1: the US Exchange Visitor visa category used for international camp staff, including the Camp Counselor and Summer Work Travel programs.
- Health form or camper health history: the medical and consent paperwork families complete before camp.

## Records and operations

- Roster: the list of campers or staff for a session, cabin, or activity.
- Bunk assignment or bunking: placing campers into cabins, often balancing age, requests, and fit.
- Incident report: the written record a camp files when an injury, behavior issue, or other problem occurs.
- Medication administration or med pass: the controlled process by which the health center gives campers their medications.
- Parent portal: the online account where families register, pay, complete forms, and get updates.
- Packing list: the camp's list of what a camper should and should not bring.
- Opening and closing day: the first and last days of a session, when campers are dropped off and picked up. Distinct from the opening and closing campfires.
