# Evening-program design

Load this when planning a campfire, a wide game, or a theme night. It has the campfire running order, the wide-game formats, the occasion guide, the wind-down, the safety and sleep checklist, and rainy-night alternatives. Adapt everything to the camp's traditions, and always run the same energy arc whether you are inside or out.

## The campfire running order (with energy ratings)

Rate each piece 1 (calm) to 5 (loud) and sequence down the scale toward the end. A starting shape:

```
Opening ceremony / lighting          energy 2-3   settle the crowd
Big group song                       energy 4     get everyone in
Loud song or cheer                   energy 5     the peak
Skit or all-camp bit                 energy 4-5
Funny song                           energy 4
Slower song                          energy 3
Story with a moral                   energy 2
Quiet closing song                   energy 1
Closing ceremony / a leader's minute energy 1     end calm
```

Aim for about 45 minutes, scaled to the youngest campers' attention. Use an emcee to host and keep it moving. In quiet songs, have campers snap instead of clap to hold the calm. Leave them wanting more.

## Wide-game formats (pick by supervision and space)

| Format | What it is | Supervision need |
|--------|-----------|------------------|
| Runaround | free movement in hard boundaries (capture the flag) | highest |
| Party / travel | campers move with their counselor and group | low, easiest across a wide area |
| Station / rotational | groups rotate through fixed stations (carnival) | medium |
| Performance | many watch a few (talent show, skits) | lowest |

Brief leaders first, then small groups. Give rules in stages. Wrap it in a loose story (characters, a goal, a soundtrack) to make it memorable. For the games themselves, see activity-planner.

## Theme nights

- Plant one repeating element across the whole day (a chant, a scoreboard, a check-in question) that builds to an evening reveal or finale.
- Run small qualifiers or auditions during the day, then stage the payoff at night.
- Staff energy and costumes are the biggest driver of whether it lands.

## Occasion guide

- **First night:** low-stakes and welcoming. Name games, easy group play, nothing high-pressure or scary while new campers settle.
- **Mid-session:** the big theme night or wide game, once the camp knows each other.
- **Last night:** reflective and a little emotional. Lean on traditions and mark the session together.

## The wind-down to bed

1. Walk back to the cabins calm, not at a sprint.
2. Lights off; campers use flashlights to find pajamas; quiet music on.
3. A low-key close: highs of the day, a short reflection or devotions, a calm story.
4. If the night had scares, end soft (a joke, a gentle song, s'mores) before sleep.

Do not end on a high; the energy has to come down so kids can fall asleep.

## Safety and sleep checklist

- [ ] Any fire is handled by a designated, trained adult under the camp's fire policy and current local burn rules. No fire-handling steps here, and never declare it safe to burn.
- [ ] An adult is present and actively supervising; cabin checks are run; the rule of three holds; overnight ratios are met.
- [ ] Hard boundaries and one clear stop signal for any runaround game.
- [ ] Scare level matches the youngest campers present; young children do not end on peak fear.
- [ ] The program ends in time for bedtime, and ends calm. Sleep is protected.
- [ ] Inclusion planned in: universal adaptations, no elimination, instructions that do not assume running or standing.

## Rainy-night alternatives (same arc, indoors)

Have one ready before the night, not during it:

- Minute-to-win-it challenges, indoor relays or an obstacle course.
- A scavenger hunt or station carnival.
- An indoor talent or skit show.
- A wide game converted to the party/travel or station format.

Run them down the same energy arc, peaking in the middle and ending calm.
