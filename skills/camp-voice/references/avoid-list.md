# Avoid-list: words, phrases, and patterns that flag AI or filler

Run a draft against this before handing copy over. The goal is copy that reads like a person at the camp wrote it. Use the plainer replacement, or cut the word. Spelling follows the camp's location (US or Canadian).

## Em dashes (the clearest tell)

AI overuses the em dash (the long dash). Most people rarely type one. Replace it:

| When you'd reach for one | Use instead |
|------------|-----|
| A pause between clauses | comma: The cabins, all lakeside, sleep eight |
| Introducing a list or explanation | colon: One price covers everything: meals, lodging, activities |
| A non-essential aside | parentheses: Sessions (one to four weeks) fit any schedule |

Reserve the em dash for rare, deliberate emphasis. More than one on a page means revise.

## Overused verbs

| Avoid | Use |
|-------|-----|
| leverage | use, draw on |
| utilize | use |
| facilitate | help, run, support |
| foster | build, encourage, grow |
| optimize | improve |
| streamline | simplify |
| enhance | improve, add to |
| unveil | show, introduce |
| navigate | handle, work through |
| empower | let, help, give |

## Overused adjectives

| Avoid | Use |
|-------|-----|
| robust | strong, reliable |
| comprehensive | full, complete |
| seamless | smooth, easy |
| innovative / cutting-edge | new |
| transformative | real, lasting |
| world-class / premier | a specific proof point instead |
| vibrant / dynamic | a concrete detail instead |
| breathtaking / stunning / idyllic / pristine | name what is actually there (a lake, pine woods) |
| nestled / tucked away | just say where it is |
| state-of-the-art | what it actually is, or how new it is |

## Transitions and connectors

| Avoid | Use |
|-------|-----|
| furthermore, moreover | also, and |
| that being said | but, still |
| in today's [world / digital age] | today, now (or cut) |
| at its core | basically (or cut) |
| it's worth noting that | note that (or cut) |

## AI phrase patterns

Opening lines to avoid: "In today's fast-paced world," "In an era of," "Imagine a world where," "It's important to note that."

Closers to avoid: "In conclusion," "At the end of the day," "By [doing X], you can [Y]."

## Structural and rhythmic tells (the ones word checks miss)

These are about sentence shape, not vocabulary, so no word list catches them. In 2026 they are the clearest sign a machine wrote the text. Every example below is a real mistake from drafting these skills.

- **Rhetorical antithesis.** "X, not Y" or "It's not just X, it's Y" used for effect. A plain contrast is fine ("use their words, not industry terms"); the tell is when the contrast is the whole point, like "This is craft, not a channel." Fix: state it plainly. "This skill is about how copy reads."
- **Balanced clauses and the rule of three.** Two or three matching clauses, often capped by a short fragment: "Warmth earns attention; specifics and proof earn the deposit. Hype loses both." Fix: break the symmetry into plain sentences. "Warmth keeps a parent reading. Specific, honest detail earns the booking."
- **Clipped fragments for effect.** A short verbless line dropped in to sound weighty: "Hype loses both." Fold it into a full sentence.
- **Grand abstractions.** "the honest core," "at its heart," "in an era of." Fix: name the concrete thing.
- **Compression for cleverness.** An idea squeezed into a pithy line the reader has to decode: "Kids pull; parents decide and pay." Fix: say it straight, or cut it if a nearby sentence already covers it.
- **Opening on a negative frame.** Leading with the reader's anxiety: "the kind a worried parent can trust." Fix: lead on what you want and drop the loaded word: "the kind a parent can trust."
- **The "whether you're X, Y, or Z" list**, and starting a sentence with "By" plus an -ing verb ("By choosing us, you...").

## Promotional and inflated phrasing

Marketing copy and AI both reach for these. They sound impressive and say nothing.

- **Tourism-brochure superlatives** (breathtaking, pristine, idyllic, premier). Replace with the concrete thing. Not "our breathtaking, pristine lakefront," but "a spring-fed lake you can swim in from the dock."
- **Significance inflation**: "a pivotal moment," "a defining summer," "a transformative experience." Say what actually happens and let the parent judge how much it matters.
- **"Boasts," "features," "offers," "serves as."** AI dodges plain "is" and "has." "The camp boasts a 40-acre site and features modern cabins" becomes "The camp sits on 40 acres, with cabins built in [year]."
- **Gerund stacking**: a trio of "-ing" outcomes like "building confidence, making lifelong friends, and creating memories." It is the rule of three again. Name one or two real things instead.
- **Vague attributions**: "studies show," "experts agree," "research proves," with no source. Name the source (the ACA, a specific study) or cut the claim. This is also an honesty rule.

## Tells specific to emails and replies

When drafting a message a person would send (see camp-emails, camp-social):

- No sycophantic openers: "Great question!", "Absolutely!", "I'd be happy to help."
- No chatbot closers: "Let me know if you have any questions!", "Feel free to reach out." End the way a person ends a note.
- No emoji unless the camp genuinely writes that way, and do not bold half the sentence. Plain text reads more human.

## Filler and empty intensifiers

Cut these or replace with something specific: absolutely, actually, basically, certainly, clearly, definitely, essentially, extremely, incredibly, literally, really, simply, truly, ultimately, very.

## Camp-marketing clichés

These are so common in camp copy that they signal nothing. Replace each with a detail only this camp could claim.

| Cliché | Replace with |
|--------|--------------|
| memories that last a lifetime | a specific tradition or moment |
| the time of their life | what they actually do that is special |
| a summer they'll never forget | a concrete outcome (made three close friends, learned to sail) |
| where friendships are made | how the cabin groups and free time actually build them |
| unplug and reconnect | the real screen-free routine |
| magical / unforgettable / fun in the sun | name the place, the activity, the people |

## Self-check

1. Read it aloud. Would you say this to a parent at pickup? If a sentence sounds like a slogan, a maxim, or a line from a keynote, rewrite it plainly.
2. Actually search the text for the em dash and en dash characters (— and –), do not just assume there are none. Any hit, including inside examples, placeholders, or bracketed asides, means it is not done. Then search for the words in the tables above.
3. Check sentence lengths vary; not every line the same shape.
4. Confirm every claim is true and every number real, or flagged as a placeholder.
5. Make sure a specific, this-camp-only detail appears early, not a generic promise.
