# Choosing and running camp activities

Load this when picking or running an activity. It has the age-band guide, the run-it sequence, inclusion patterns, a debrief frame, the safety checklist, and a few worked examples. Match every choice to the group and the moment, and treat the examples as shapes to adapt.

## Match by age band

| Age | What suits it | What to avoid |
|-----|---------------|---------------|
| 5 to 7 | simple rules, short rounds, movement, imaginative or role-play, cooperative | long rules, scorekeeping, anything needing fine coordination or patience |
| 8 to 10 | clear rules and fairness, light strategy, team belonging, skill mastery | drawn-out games, heavy peer comparison |
| 11 to 13 | choice and autonomy, team challenges, a low-exposure way in | anything that singles a kid out physically in front of peers |
| Teens | strategy, initiative tasks, leadership and co-leading, real responsibility | childish framing, busywork, being talked down to |

## Run it well: explain, demonstrate, guide, enable

1. **Explain** the overview simply. Do not front-load every rule; give enough to start.
2. **Demonstrate** it, ideally with a quick walk-through round.
3. **Guide** the first real round, coaching and correcting live.
4. **Enable** them to play and own it.

Set the tone, the boundaries, and the safety rules before the first round. Pitch instructions to the youngest player in the group.

## Inclusion patterns

- **No elimination, or fast re-entry.** If a game has "out," add a way back in: rotate, switch roles, pair up, or run a rolling side-game so no one waits long.
- **Adapt the whole game, not the child.** If one camper needs a change (a noodle instead of a hand, a walk instead of a run), give everyone that version.
- **Graded entry.** Offer a lower-intensity way to take part for a nervous or less-able camper.
- **Inclusive instructions.** "Meet me at the line," not "race to the line." Do not assume everyone can run, stand, or see.
- **No captains picking teams.** Count off, draw, or pre-sort instead, so no one is chosen last.

## Debrief, when there is a goal

If the activity is meant to build teamwork, trust, or a lesson, process it briefly afterward:

- **What** happened?
- **So what** does that tell us?
- **Now what** will we carry into next time?

Keep it quick and concrete for younger campers; go deeper with teens.

## Safety checklist

- **Ratios:** enough staff to hold the camp's ratio for that age, at every concurrent group. Ratios are set by the camp's jurisdiction and accreditor.
- **High-risk activities** (water, heights, projectiles, animals): certified staff, the right equipment, and the tighter ratios their own standards require. Never run these as general games.
- **Equipment:** checked for condition and age-appropriateness before play.
- **Heat:** hydration, shade, and breaks built in; watch for heat-illness signs; follow the camp's and local heat policy for cutoffs.
- **Allergies and medical:** screened before any activity involving food, animals, the outdoors, water, or exertion; know the camp's emergency procedure.
- **Emotional safety:** no public humiliation, no forced performance, a graded way in for anyone who needs it.

## Named games to draw from

For the actual games, use `references/activity-bank.md`: each is named, tagged by age, energy, group size, space, and gear, carries a way to keep every camper in play, and traces to a current authoritative source. The four shapes a counselor reaches for most, with bank picks for each:

- **A calm wind-down** (low energy, settling, before bed or after a meal): Line of Silence, Huckle Buckle Beanstalk, Up Down Stop Go.
- **A big-group field game** (high energy, outdoor, hard boundaries): Sharks and Minnows, Everyone's It Tag, Capture the Flag.
- **A small-group circle or name game** (settling, learning names early in a session): Movement Name Game, Boppity Bop Bop Bop, I Love My Neighbor.
- **A no-equipment team task** (teens, team-building, debrief-worthy): All Tangled Up, Alligator Swamp, Grid Minefield.

Match the pick to the group using the age-band guide and the situational fit above, never by the shape alone.
